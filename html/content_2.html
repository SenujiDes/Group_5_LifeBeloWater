MARINE POLLUTION

Marine pollution refers to the introduction of harmful substances or materials into the ocean, seas, or other bodies of water, resulting in adverse effects on marine life and ecosystems. These pollutants can come from various sources, including industrial discharge, agricultural runoff, oil spills, plastic waste, and sewage. Marine pollution can lead to habitat destruction, harm to marine organisms, contamination of seafood, and disruptions to the entire marine food chain. It poses significant environmental, economic, and health risks, making it a critical issue that requires global attention and concerted efforts to mitigate and prevent further damage to our oceans.

MARINE PLASTIC POLLUTION

Plastic is a synthetic, organic polymer made from petroleum with properties ideally suited for a wide variety of applications including: packaging, building and construction, household and sports equipment, vehicles, electronics, and agriculture. Over 300 million tons of plastic are produced every year, half of which is used to create single-use items such as shopping bags, cups and straws. If discarded improperly, plastic waste can harm the environment and biodiversity.

The main sources of plastic debris found in the ocean are land-based, coming from urban and stormwater runoff, sewer overflows, littering, inadequate waste disposal and management, industrial activities, tyre abrasion, construction and illegal dumping. Ocean-based plastic pollution originates primarily from the fishing industry, nautical activities and aquaculture.

Under the influence of solar UV radiation, wind, currents and other natural factors, plastic breaks down into small particles called microplastics (particles smaller than 5 mm) or nanoplastics (particles smaller than 100 nm). The small size makes them easy for marine life to ingest accidentally.

How this affect to the Humans

Impacts on food and human health

Microplastics have been found in tap water, beer, salt and are present in all samples collected in the world’s oceans, including the Arctic. Several chemicals used in the production of plastic materials are known to be carcinogenic and to interfere with the body’s endocrine system, causing developmental, reproductive, neurological, and immune disorders in both humans and wildlife. Recently, microplastics were found in human placentas but more research is needed to determine if this is a widespread problem.

Toxic contaminants also accumulate on the surface of plastic as a result of prolonged exposure to seawater. When marine organisms ingest plastic debris, these contaminants enter their digestive systems, and over time accumulate in the food web. The transfer of contaminants between marine species and humans through consumption of seafood has been identified as a health hazard, and research is ongoing.

Impacts on climate change

Marine plastic pollution has multifaceted impacts on climate change, stemming from its lifecycle and interactions with the environment. Firstly, the production of plastic entails the extraction and processing of fossil fuels, leading to substantial greenhouse gas emissions. From drilling oil for raw materials to refining and manufacturing processes, each stage releases carbon dioxide and other pollutants into the atmosphere, exacerbating global warming. Additionally, the disposal of plastic waste further contributes to emissions. Incineration, a common method of waste management, releases carbon dioxide and methane, both potent greenhouse gases, into the air.

Beyond emissions, marine plastics affect the ocean's ability to regulate climate through various mechanisms. Plastics alter the carbon cycle by absorbing and releasing gases, disrupting the exchange of carbon dioxide between the ocean and the atmosphere. This disturbance can impede the ocean's capacity to sequester carbon, exacerbating the greenhouse effect. Moreover, large accumulations of plastic debris alter oceanic dynamics. They can disrupt currents and circulation patterns, which play crucial roles in redistributing heat and nutrients across the globe. Alterations to these processes can have cascading effects on climate systems, influencing weather patterns and ocean temperatures.

Furthermore, marine plastics impact ecosystems that are integral to climate regulation. Coral reefs, for instance, provide habitat for diverse marine life and support fisheries while sequestering carbon dioxide. Plastics pose a threat to coral health, leading to degradation and loss of these vital ecosystems. Similarly, microplastics are ingested by marine organisms, entering the food chain and potentially bioaccumulating harmful chemicals. This disrupts the balance of marine ecosystems, affecting carbon cycling and nutrient dynamics, which are essential for regulating climate.

In summary, marine plastic pollution contributes to climate change through its lifecycle emissions, disruption of oceanic processes, and degradation of ecosystems crucial for climate regulation. Addressing this issue requires comprehensive strategies to reduce plastic production, improve waste management practices, and mitigate the impacts on marine environments. By addressing marine plastic pollution, we can help mitigate climate change and preserve the health of our oceans for future generations.

Impacts on tourism

Plastic waste damages the aesthetic value of tourist destinations, leading to decreased income from tourism. It also generates major economic costs related to the cleaning and maintenance of the sites. The build-up of plastic litter on beaches can have a negative impact on a country’s economy, wildlife, and the physical and psychological wellbeing of people.

Why is this important?

Plastic pollution is a widespread problem affecting the marine environment. It threatens ocean health, the health of marine species, food safety and quality, human health, coastal tourism, and contributes to climate change.

MARINE OIL POLLUTION

Marine oil pollution presents significant challenges and requires comprehensive solutions to mitigate its environmental impacts. Firstly, prevention is crucial. Enhanced regulations and enforcement measures can reduce the likelihood of oil spills from offshore drilling, shipping, and other industrial activities. Stringent safety standards for oil tankers, pipelines, and drilling rigs can minimize the risk of accidents and leaks. Additionally, investing in research and development of safer and more efficient technologies for oil extraction, transportation, and storage can help prevent spills. In the event of an oil spill, swift and effective response measures are essential to minimize environmental damage. Establishing rapid response teams equipped with specialized equipment and training can ensure quick containment and cleanup of spilled oil. Utilizing booms, skimmers, and dispersants can help contain and remove oil from the water surface, while bioremediation techniques can accelerate the natural degradation of oil by microorganisms. Moreover, promoting international cooperation and coordination is crucial for addressing transboundary oil pollution incidents. Establishing agreements and protocols for mutual assistance and information sharing can facilitate a coordinated response to oil spills across borders. Collaborative efforts between governments, industries, and non-governmental organizations can enhance preparedness and response capabilities on a global scale. Long-term monitoring and restoration efforts are also necessary to mitigate the lasting effects of oil pollution on marine ecosystems. Monitoring programs can assess the extent of environmental damage and track the recovery of affected habitats and species over time. Implementing restoration projects, such as habitat rehabilitation and reforestation of coastal areas, can help restore ecosystem resilience and biodiversity. Furthermore, holding polluters accountable through strict liability laws and penalties can serve as a deterrent against negligent practices and encourage investment in pollution prevention measures. Financial incentives, such as pollution liability insurance and pollution taxes, can also incentivize companies to adopt cleaner and safer technologies. Overall, addressing marine oil pollution requires a multi-faceted approach encompassing prevention, preparedness, response, restoration, and accountability. By implementing comprehensive solutions and fostering international collaboration, we can mitigate the environmental impacts of oil pollution and safeguard marine ecosystems for future generations.

How this affect to the Humans

Contamination of Seafood

Oil spills can contaminate seafood with toxic substances such as polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and heavy metals. Consumption of contaminated seafood can lead to adverse health effects in humans, including gastrointestinal issues, liver damage, and increased risk of cancer. This poses a significant threat to communities that rely on fishing as a primary food source, particularly in coastal regions.

Health Risks from Cleanup Efforts

Workers involved in oil spill cleanup operations are exposed to hazardous chemicals present in oil and dispersants, putting them at risk of respiratory problems, skin irritation, and other health issues. Improper handling of contaminated materials or inadequate protective gear can exacerbate these risks, highlighting the importance of proper safety protocols and training for cleanup personnel.

Impact on Recreational Activities:

Oil pollution can degrade the aesthetic and recreational value of coastal areas, impacting tourism and recreational activities such as swimming, boating, and beachcombing. Direct contact with contaminated water or shoreline debris can pose health risks to recreational users, particularly children who may inadvertently ingest or come into contact with oil residues.

Why is this important?

Addressing marine oil pollution is vital for preserving ecosystems, safeguarding human health, sustaining economies reliant on marine resources, mitigating climate change, and fostering international cooperation. Effective measures are needed to prevent spills, minimize impacts, and promote a sustainable future for coastal communities and marine environments.

What can be done?

Efforts should be made to adhere to and strengthen existing international legislative frameworks that address marine plastic pollution. The most important are the 1972 Convention on the Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping Wastes and Other Matter (the London Convention), the 1996 Protocol to the London Convention (the London Protocol) and the 1978 Protocol to the International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships (MARPOL).

Regional and national governments should also explore national legislative frameworks on Extended Producer Responsibility. These are emerging as innovative, low-cost solutions, as are policies to promote circular economies.

Governments, research institutions and industries need to work collaboratively to redesign products, and rethink their use and disposal to reduce microplastic waste from pellets, synthetic textiles and tyres. Consumers and society must shift to more sustainable consumption patterns. This will require solutions which go beyond waste management and consider the whole lifecycle of plastic products; from design to infrastructure, and household use.

More funding for research and innovation should be made available to provide policymakers, manufacturers and consumers with the evidence needed to implement technological, behavioural and policy solutions to address marine plastic pollution.

How we can protect marine

Protecting marine environments necessitates a comprehensive and concerted effort that addresses various sources of stress and degradation. Firstly, reducing plastic pollution is paramount, necessitating both individual actions and systemic changes. Efforts to minimize plastic waste through the use of reusable products, proper waste management, and policies targeting single-use plastics are crucial. Additionally, combating climate change is essential, as rising temperatures and ocean acidification pose significant threats to marine ecosystems. Transitioning to renewable energy sources, promoting energy efficiency, and conserving coastal habitats that sequester carbon can help mitigate these impacts and safeguard marine environments.

Furthermore, preventing pollution from industrial activities, shipping, and runoff is vital for maintaining water quality and ecosystem health. This requires strict enforcement of regulations, investment in wastewater treatment infrastructure, and sustainable land use practices to minimize pollution inputs into marine environments. Additionally, protecting marine protected areas and effectively managing them can conserve biodiversity and provide refuges for vulnerable species. Through these concerted efforts, along with promoting environmental education, supporting sustainable tourism, and investing in research and monitoring, we can work towards preserving marine environments for future generations.
